# Soteriology

### Q52. Are spiritually dead sinners, considered in themselves, morally unable to come to Christ apart from prior efficacious grace?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This is a gateway question for debates over depravity, grace, and conversion.

- Yes: `Total inability`

- Yes proof texts: John 6:44, 65; Romans 8:7-8; 1 Corinthians 2:14; Ephesians 2:1-5

- No: `Prevenient-enabled or resistible response model`

- No proof texts: John 12:32; Titus 2:11; Romans 2:4; Acts 17:27-30

- Note: Arminian and Wesleyan traditions typically answer `No` here not because they deny the seriousness of the fall, but because they affirm a universally given prior grace that enables response. `Q52` asks about inability; `Q53` then asks whether the needed enabling grace is universal.

### Q53. Does God grant a universal prevenient grace that sufficiently counteracts total inability so that all hearers may freely respond to the gospel?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This is a central Arminian and Wesleyan account of how grace and responsibility relate.

- Yes: `Prevenient grace`

- Yes proof texts: John 12:32; Titus 2:11; Romans 2:4; Acts 17:27-30

- No: `Special efficacious grace only`

- No proof texts: John 6:37, 44; Acts 13:48; Romans 9:16

- Note: Some traditions affirm a universal work of grace while refusing the technical label `prevenient grace`, so answer according to the substance of the claim rather than the term itself.

### Q54. Is election unto salvation unconditional rather than grounded in foreseen faith?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This is one of the clearest fault lines between Reformed and Arminian systems.

- Yes: `Unconditional election`

- Yes proof texts: John 15:16; Acts 13:48; Romans 9:11-18; Ephesians 1:4-5; 2 Timothy 1:9

- No: `Conditional election`

- No proof texts: Romans 8:29; 1 Peter 1:1-2; 2 Thessalonians 2:13

- Note: `Q54` asks on what basis God elects; `Q55` then asks whether election is best framed primarily as corporate or individual.

### Q55. Is election in Christ best understood primarily as corporate, with individuals sharing in that election by union with Christ through faith?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This captures a major Arminian and some broader evangelical reading that is not fully expressed by the unconditional-versus-conditional formula alone.

- Yes: `Corporate election view`

- Yes proof texts: Ephesians 1:4; Ephesians 1:13; Galatians 3:26-29; 1 Peter 2:9-10

- No: `Primarily individual election view`

- No proof texts: John 6:37; Acts 13:48; Romans 9:11-18; Ephesians 1:4-5

- Note: Many Arminians hold that Christ is the elect one and that believers participate in that election by faith-union with him, while Reformed readings usually take these passages to teach the prior election of particular persons. `Q55` asks about the shape of election, not its basis or temporal order.

### Q56. Is God's foreknowledge in Romans 8:29 best understood relationally rather than as foresight of future faith?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This helps define what "foreknew" means in election texts.

- Yes: `Relational foreknowledge / fore-love`

- Yes proof texts: Amos 3:2; Romans 8:29; Romans 11:2; 1 Peter 1:20

- No: `Foresight of faith`

- No proof texts: Romans 8:29; 1 Peter 1:1-2

- Note: This is largely a lexical and theological debate over whether `foreknew` means fore-love, fore-ordination, or prior knowledge of future faith. It is narrower than `Q54`, which asks what election is conditioned upon.

### Q57. Did Christ die with the specific intent infallibly to save the elect?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This is one formulation of the extent and intent of the atonement debate.

- Yes: `Particular redemption`

- Yes proof texts: John 10:11, 14-15, 26-29; Acts 20:28; Ephesians 5:25; Matthew 1:21

- No: `Universal provision`

- No proof texts: John 3:16; 1 Timothy 2:5-6; Hebrews 2:9; 1 John 2:2; 2 Peter 2:1

- Note: This question asks about the design or intent of the atonement. `Q58` then asks what the cross actually secured in application.

### Q58. Did Christ's atoning death itself secure the saving faith and final salvation of those for whom he died?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This sharpens the difference between an atonement that actually purchases the application of redemption and one that principally makes salvation possible upon a later faith response.

- Yes: `Definite atonement with definite application`

- Yes proof texts: John 6:37-39; John 10:15-16; Ephesians 5:25-27; Hebrews 10:14

- No: `Universal provision with conditional application`

- No proof texts: 1 Timothy 2:5-6; Hebrews 2:9; 1 John 2:2; 2 Peter 2:1

- Note: The classic formula `sufficient for all, efficient for the elect` is used in more than one tradition, so this question asks instead whether the cross itself secured the saving application for a definite people. It is about efficacy in application, not merely design in intention.

### Q59. Is the outward gospel call to all hearers a sincere expression of God's real summons to repent and believe?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This affects preaching, evangelism, and the theology of divine desire.

- Yes: `Free offer of the gospel`

- Yes proof texts: Isaiah 55:1; Ezekiel 18:23, 32; Matthew 11:28; Acts 17:30

- No: `High Calvinist reservation regarding universal well-meant offer`

- No proof texts: John 6:37, 44; Romans 9:18; John 10:26

- Note: Most evangelical traditions, including most Reformed traditions, answer `Yes` here. The `No` answer is usually inferred from particularist election and redemption texts rather than from an explicit denial of the universal external summons. `Q59` concerns the external call; `Q60` concerns the internal or effectual call.

### Q60. Is saving grace in the effectual call finally irresistible to the elect?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This is the classic debate over effectual calling and resistible grace.

- Yes: `Irresistible grace / effectual calling`

- Yes proof texts: John 6:37, 44; Romans 8:30; Acts 16:14; Ezekiel 36:26-27

- No: `Resistible grace`

- No proof texts: Matthew 23:37; Luke 7:30; Acts 7:51; Hebrews 10:29

- Note: `Q60` asks whether the inward saving call can finally be resisted, not whether people often resist outward preaching and command.

### Q61. Does regeneration logically precede and produce saving faith?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This is a core ordo salutis question.

- Yes: `Regeneration before faith`

- Yes proof texts: John 1:12-13; John 3:3-8; 1 John 5:1; Ephesians 2:4-5

- No: `Faith before regeneration`

- No proof texts: John 20:31; Acts 16:31; Galatians 3:2; Ephesians 1:13

- Note: Here `precede` refers to logical order in the application of salvation, not necessarily a measurable temporal gap. `Q61` asks about order; `Q62` asks whether faith should itself be described as a specially granted gift.

### Q62. Is faith itself a special gift granted only to the elect?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This affects how faith is understood as gift, command, and human act.

- Yes: `Monergistic gift-of-faith view`

- Yes proof texts: John 6:65; Acts 18:27; Philippians 1:29; 2 Peter 1:1

- No: `Faith as commanded human response enabled by grace`

- No proof texts: Mark 1:15; Acts 17:30; Romans 10:9-10, 17

- Note: Ephesians 2:8-9 is often cited in this debate, but many interpreters dispute whether the grammar there identifies faith specifically as the gift; it is omitted here to keep the list tighter. A person may answer `No` here while still answering `Yes` to `Q61`, depending on how narrowly `gift` language is being used.

### Q63. Is repentance divinely granted rather than solely self-generated?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This affects conversion, preaching, and the doctrine of grace.

- Yes: `Repentance as gift`

- Yes proof texts: Acts 5:31; Acts 11:18; 2 Timothy 2:25

- No: `Repentance as commanded human response`

- No proof texts: Mark 1:15; Acts 2:38; Acts 17:30

### Q64. Is justification by faith alone the exclusive instrument of receiving Christ's righteousness?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This stands near the center of Protestant soteriology.

- Yes: `Sola fide`

- Yes proof texts: Romans 3:28; Romans 4:5; Galatians 2:16; Philippians 3:9

- No: `Final justification includes works in more than evidential sense`

- No proof texts: Matthew 25:31-46; Romans 2:6-13; James 2:24

- Note: Some traditions affirm initial justification by faith alone while still speaking of a final justification according to works in a stronger sense than mere evidence. Answer according to the full system you find most persuasive, not merely the initial moment of conversion. `Q64` asks about the instrument of justification; `Q65` asks about the content or mode of the righteousness received.

### Q65. Is Christ's righteousness imputed to believers rather than righteousness being only transformative or covenantal-status language?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This clarifies how justification, union, and righteousness are related.

- Yes: `Imputed righteousness`

- Yes proof texts: Romans 4:5-8; Romans 5:17-19; 2 Corinthians 5:21; Philippians 3:9

- No: `Transformative or covenantal-status emphasis`

- No proof texts: Jeremiah 31:33-34; Romans 2:13; James 2:24

- Note: The `No` answer need not deny forensic categories altogether; it usually denies that justification is best explained by the imputation of Christ's active and/or total righteousness as a distinct legal transfer. It is also more synthetic and inferential than the `Yes` case, since no single text explicitly says, in so many words, that Christ's righteousness is not imputed. `Q65` is about the nature of justifying righteousness, not about whether union with Christ is broader than justification.

### Q66. Should union with Christ be treated as the broader reality from which justification, adoption, and sanctification flow?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This determines whether salvation benefits are organized around union or primarily around a linear order of salvation.

- Yes: `Union-with-Christ priority`

- Yes proof texts: John 15:1-7; Romans 6:3-11; 1 Corinthians 1:30; Ephesians 1:3-14

- No: `Ordo salutis distinctions primary`

- No proof texts: Romans 8:29-30; 1 Corinthians 6:11; Titus 3:5-7

- Note: `Q66` asks for the organizing center of salvation language. It does not deny that one may still distinguish justification, adoption, and sanctification.

### Q67. Is assurance of salvation ordinarily attainable in the Christian life, even if it is not always constant?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This shapes pastoral care, self-examination, and the relation of faith and assurance.

- Yes: `Ordinary assurance attainable`

- Yes proof texts: Romans 8:15-16, 38-39; 1 John 5:13; Hebrews 10:22

- No: `Assurance may be uncertain or intermittent`

- No proof texts: 2 Corinthians 13:5; Hebrews 6:4-8; 2 Peter 1:10

- Note: This question is not identical to asking whether assurance is of the essence of saving faith. Some traditions deny the latter while still strongly affirming the ordinary attainability of assurance. `Q67` asks whether assurance can ordinarily be had; `Q68` asks what should chiefly ground it.

### Q68. Should assurance ordinarily be grounded chiefly in Christ's promise and the Spirit's present witness rather than in retrospective evidence of perseverance?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This distinguishes traditions that locate assurance primarily in present faith and divine testimony from those that emphasize evidences, fruit, and self-examination more heavily.

- Yes: `Promise-and-witness emphasis`

- Yes proof texts: Romans 8:15-16; Galatians 4:6; 1 John 5:10-13

- No: `Evidence-and-perseverance emphasis`

- No proof texts: Matthew 7:16-20; 2 Corinthians 13:5; 2 Peter 1:10

- Note: Most evangelical traditions use both promise and evidences. The question asks which should ordinarily function as the primary ground of assurance, not whether the secondary ground has any place.

### Q69. Will all true believers certainly persevere to final salvation?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This is the perseverance versus conditional security debate in its clearest form.

- Yes: `Perseverance of the saints`

- Yes proof texts: John 10:27-29; Romans 8:30-39; Philippians 1:6; 1 Peter 1:5; 1 John 2:19

- No: `Conditional security`

- No proof texts: John 15:6; Hebrews 6:4-6; Hebrews 10:26-29; 2 Peter 2:20-22

- Note: Some traditions answer `No` because they believe genuinely regenerate believers may apostatize, while others answer `Yes` because they distinguish temporary faith or covenant participation from final regeneration. `Q69` asks about the final outcome; `Q70` asks whether justification itself can be reversed.

### Q70. Can a person who has been truly justified later become unjustified through unbelief or apostasy?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This makes explicit a distinction that is often assumed but not stated in debates over perseverance and apostasy.

- Yes: `Justification can be forfeited`

- Yes proof texts: Galatians 5:4; John 15:6; Romans 11:20-22; Hebrews 10:38-39

- No: `Justification is irreversible`

- No proof texts: Romans 5:1; Romans 8:30, 33-34; John 10:28-29

- Note: Some Arminian traditions speak more readily of falling from saving union or grace than of `becoming unjustified`, but the practical question is whether a truly justified person can later forfeit that standing. This is a more specific claim than `Q69`.

### Q71. Do the major warning passages describe genuinely regenerate believers who can finally fall away?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This question helps interpret difficult warning texts and apparent apostasy.

- Yes: `Warning passages refer to regenerate believers`

- Yes proof texts: Hebrews 6:4-6; Hebrews 10:29; 2 Peter 2:20-22

- No: `Warning passages describe covenant participants or professors, not finally regenerate persons`

- No proof texts: 1 John 2:19; John 10:28-29; Luke 8:13

- Note: The central dispute is not whether the warnings are real, but whether the persons warned are regenerate in the fullest salvific sense. On the `No` side, Luke 8:13 is typically read as describing temporary faith rather than regeneration proper. `Q71` is exegetical: it asks how the warning texts should be read, not only what system they are later fitted into.

### Q72. Is sanctification decisively initiated at conversion rather than subsequent to a second blessing?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This clarifies whether holiness begins definitively at conversion or through a later crisis event.

- Yes: `Definitive sanctification at conversion`

- Yes proof texts: 1 Corinthians 1:2; 1 Corinthians 6:11; Hebrews 10:10, 14

- No: `Second-blessing sanctification emphasis`

- No proof texts: Acts 8:14-17; Acts 19:1-6; 1 Thessalonians 5:23

- Note: The `No` answer is argued chiefly from certain readings of Acts and holiness theology, not from a single text that explicitly describes a universal second blessing after conversion. `Q72` asks when sanctification begins; `Q73` asks how complete sanctification may become in this life.

### Q73. Is entire sanctification or freedom from willful sin possible in this life?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This is a key Wesleyan-holiness and Reformed/Baptist dividing line.

- Yes: `Entire sanctification / victory emphasis`

- Yes proof texts: Romans 6:6; 1 Thessalonians 5:23-24; 1 John 3:6, 9; 1 John 5:18

- No: `Ongoing sin remains in believers`

- No proof texts: Philippians 3:12; 1 John 1:8; James 3:2

- Note: The dispute often turns on how `sin`, `perfection`, and `entire sanctification` are defined, especially in Johannine and Wesleyan readings.

### Q74. Is baptism the normative public response closely tied to conversion?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This affects how repentance, faith, baptism, and conversion are pastorally related.

- Yes: `Conversion-baptism normativity`

- Yes proof texts: Acts 2:38; Acts 8:36-38; Acts 22:16; 1 Peter 3:21

- No: `Baptism important but not conversion-constitutive`

- No proof texts: Luke 23:43; 1 Corinthians 1:17; Romans 10:9-10; Acts 10:44-48

- Note: The `Yes` answer here concerns normativity and close association, not necessarily baptismal regeneration. Mark 16:16 is omitted because of the well-known text-critical dispute over the longer ending of Mark.

### Q75. Should justification and final judgment according to works be harmonized by treating works as evidential fruit rather than co-instrumental cause?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This addresses one of the most difficult intersections in biblical soteriology.

- Yes: `Works as evidence, not instrument`

- Yes proof texts: Ephesians 2:8-10; Titus 3:5-8; James 2:17-18

- No: `Final justification through faithfulness or obedient perseverance`

- No proof texts: Romans 2:6-13; Matthew 25:31-46; James 2:24

- Note: This question is partly terminological. Some traditions use `justification` differently at the initial and final stages, while others prefer to reserve `justification` for the once-for-all declarative act and speak of final vindication instead. The key issue is whether works are evidential, instrumental, or covenantally necessary for final acquittal. It therefore develops `Q64` rather than repeating it.

### Q76. Is adoption a distinct salvific benefit rather than only another name for justification?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This affects the structure of salvation and the place of family language within redemption.

- Yes: `Adoption as distinct benefit`

- Yes proof texts: John 1:12; Romans 8:15-17; Galatians 4:4-7; 1 John 3:1

- No: `Adoption subsumed under union or justification`

- No proof texts: Galatians 3:26-29; Ephesians 1:5

- Note: The `No` answer is more a systematic-theological compression than a direct exegetical claim, since Scripture more often presents adoption positively than argues whether it should be treated as a distinct locus.

### Q77. Is a fixed order of salvation the best way to summarize the application of redemption?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This determines whether salvation is best described as a structured sequence or a cluster of inseparable benefits.

- Yes: `Strong ordo salutis`

- Yes proof texts: Romans 8:30; John 3:3-8; Ephesians 2:4-10

- No: `Less rigid, more integrated application of redemption`

- No proof texts: John 20:31; Acts 16:31; Galatians 3:2; Ephesians 1:13

- Note: Even traditions answering `Yes` often treat the order as logical rather than temporal. Even traditions answering `No` usually still distinguish the major benefits of salvation. `Q77` asks about summary method, not whether any distinctions can be made.

### Q78. Does divine sovereignty in salvation operate compatibilistically with meaningful human responsibility?

- Category: Soteriology

- Answer: [ ] Yes  [ ] No

- Why this matters: This question often summarizes how a whole soteriological system holds together.

- Yes: `Compatibilism`

- Yes proof texts: Acts 2:23; Philippians 2:12-13; Genesis 50:20

- No: `Libertarian synergy`

- No proof texts: Deuteronomy 30:19; Joshua 24:15; Matthew 23:37

- Note: The `No` answer does not imply a denial of divine sovereignty, only a different account of how sovereignty and human willing are related.
